# B2C — Lifecycle Email Starter Prompts

> **Context:** B2C lifecycle email is behavior-triggered, brand-voice consistent, and relationship-first. The highest-performing sequences feel personal, even at scale. Key sequences: welcome series (sets the brand relationship tone), post-purchase (confirms the buyer's identity decision), win-back (earned, not desperate). Prompts here are designed for consumer email programs that build loyalty, not just drive transactions.

## Prompts

### 1. Welcome Email Series — New Subscriber (3 Emails)

Write a 3-email welcome series for a B2C brand targeting a new email subscriber who has not yet made a purchase.

**Email 1 (Immediately after signup — Day 0):**
- Subject: Creative, brand-voice — not "Welcome to [Brand]!"
- Body (150 words): Deliver the signup offer (if applicable). Introduce the brand in 2–3 sentences — the "why" behind the brand, not the feature list. Personal and warm, not a terms-and-conditions email.
- CTA: One action — shop the most relevant collection or read the brand story.
- Tone: Brand-voice consistent, like a friend introducing the brand to them.

**Email 2 (Day 2–3 — Brand Story / Social Proof):**
- Subject: Hook on a specific product benefit or customer story
- Body (150–180 words): Share one customer story or UGC highlight — how a real customer uses the product, with a specific benefit. This is not a review summary — it's a story that helps the subscriber visualize ownership.
- CTA: "See what others are saying" → reviews page, or "Shop the collection."
- Tone: Social proof-led, peer trust.

**Email 3 (Day 5–7 — Purchase Nudge):**
- Subject: Soft urgency or personalized angle (e.g., "Still thinking it over?")
- Body (100–120 words): Acknowledge they haven't bought yet (lightly, without being pushy). Reinforce one key benefit or differentiator. If there's an introductory offer expiring, mention it honestly.
- CTA: Single CTA — "Explore [Brand]" or "See the collection."
- Tone: Warm, low-pressure. The goal is to feel like a reminder from a brand you already like, not a re-marketing ad.

---

### 2. Post-Purchase Email — First-Time Buyer

Write a post-purchase email sent 2–3 days after a first-time order (not the order confirmation email — this is the relationship-building email).

Requirements:
- Subject: No "Your order shipped" subject line. This is an emotional follow-up.
- Opening (50 words): Acknowledge the purchase as a decision they'll love — not a transaction. Specific to the product type ordered.
- Body (100–120 words): First-use tip or insight about getting the most from the product. One customer story or review that reinforces their decision ("Others who ordered [product] said…").
- Optional: Invite them to share their experience (review request framed as community, not compliance).
- CTA: One action — care guide, first-use tip article, or community page.
- Tone: Celebratory, peer-level, brand-warm. NOT a discount upsell.

---

### 3. Loyalty Reward Email — Milestone Recognition

Write a B2C loyalty email for a customer who has just hit a milestone (e.g., 5th purchase, 1-year anniversary, or accumulated [X] loyalty points).

Requirements:
- Subject: Make it feel like recognition, not a promotion
- Opening (40 words): Name the specific milestone — "You've been with us for a year" or "That's your 5th order." Specific beats generic.
- Middle (80 words): What this milestone means to the brand — genuine, brief, not sentimental-for-the-sake-of-it. Include the reward or surprise (discount, early access, free addition to next order).
- CTA: Redeem or explore the reward — specific and easy.
- Tone: Grateful, brand-voice warm, celebratory without feeling like a promotional email wearing a costume.

---

### 4. Win-Back Email — Lapsed Customer (90+ Days No Purchase)

Write a win-back email for a B2C customer who hasn't purchased in 90+ days.

Requirements:
- Subject: Acknowledge the absence without being guilt-inducing. Options: playful ("We've been wondering where you went"), genuinely curious ("What happened to you?"), or offer-led ("We saved something for you").
- Body (100–130 words): Don't launch into product features. Start by acknowledging the gap. Then one genuine reason to come back: new product, something they loved is restocked, the brand has done something notable since they last visited.
- Offer (optional): A win-back offer if the brand's economics support it — but not mandatory. A genuine reconnect email without discount can outperform a discount email in long-term LTV.
- Final line: Permission to leave gracefully — "If the timing isn't right, no problem. Here's how to manage your email preferences."
- Tone: Brand-voice authentic, not desperate. The goal is reconnection, not transaction recovery.
