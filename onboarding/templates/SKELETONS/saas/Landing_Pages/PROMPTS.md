# SaaS — Landing Pages Starter Prompts

> **Context:** SaaS landing pages serve self-serve and sales-assisted acquisition. Trial-removing friction is the primary mechanic — the page should answer "will this work for me?" before asking anything of the visitor. Emphasis on: outcome-first headlines, integration proof, pricing transparency, and low-commitment CTAs. Prompts here cover free trial, pricing, integration-specific, and homepage hero patterns.

## Prompts

### 1. Free Trial / Signup Landing Page

Write a free trial signup landing page for a SaaS product. The goal is to convert product-aware visitors who landed from paid or organic to start a trial without friction.

Page structure to generate:

**Hero section:**
- H1 (outcome-led, 8–12 words): What the user achieves, not what the product does.
- Subheadline (1–2 sentences): How the product achieves it. Platform/use-case specificity.
- Primary CTA button copy: "Start free" or "Try [Product] free" — never "Sign up."
- Trust signal below CTA: "No credit card required. Free for [X days/users/volume]."

**3-bullet value section (below the fold):**
- Format: Icon + bold benefit label + 12-word benefit statement.
- Cover: Setup speed, integration ease, key value delivery window ("See results in [X days]").

**Social proof strip:**
- Logos (5–7 brands) + "Trusted by [N] teams at [company types]."

**Tone:**
- Outcome-first, technically credible, friction-signaling awareness ("No credit card. 2-minute setup. Real data from day one.").
- Write for a product-aware evaluator who has already done some research.

### 2. Pricing Page Copy — 3-Tier Structure

Write the copy for a SaaS pricing page with three tiers.

Inputs:
- Tier names: [Free/Starter/Growth] or [Starter/Pro/Enterprise] — or specify.
- Target users per tier: [e.g., "individual, small team, mid-market"].
- Core job-to-be-done per tier: [what each buyer is trying to accomplish].
- 3 key distinguishing features per tier (limit these — pricing pages die by feature list volume).

Deliverable:

For each tier:
- **Tier name + price** (format: "$XX/month per seat" or "Free forever")
- **1-sentence positioning statement**: Who this is for and what it enables.
- **Feature list** (5–7 items max, job-to-be-done language, not feature names):
  - Good: "Unlimited email sequences with reply detection"
  - Bad: "ReplyTrack™ enabled"
- **CTA button**: Tier-appropriate ("Start free" / "Start trial" / "Talk to sales").

**FAQ section (4 questions):**
- "What happens at the end of my trial?"
- "Can I switch plans later?"
- "Do you offer discounts for nonprofits / startups / annual billing?"
- "What's included in Enterprise that isn't in [top paid tier]?"

Tone: Transparent about limitations, confident about value. Each tier should feel like the right choice for its buyer — not a downgrade from the tier above.

### 3. Integration-Specific Co-Marketing Landing Page

Write a landing page for a "[Product] + [Partner]" integration.

Context: This page typically ranks for "[Product] [Partner] integration" or lives at `/integrations/[partner]`. The audience already uses the partner tool and is evaluating this product or vice versa.

Page structure:

**Hero:**
- H1: "[Product] works directly with [Partner]." (Plain statement beats clever.)
- Subhead (1–2 sentences): What the connection enables — the combined workflow, not the technical bridge.
- Visual: Integration flow diagram description (describe what the image should show — two logos connected by arrows, data/workflow indicators).
- CTA: "Connect [Partner] in [Product]" — present tense implies simplicity.

**What you can do together (3–4 bullets):**
- Format: "[Action verb] [what], without [manual step]"
- Example: "Sync contacts from [Partner] to [Product] segments automatically — no CSV exports ever."

**Setup instructions (simplified prose, not numbered tech docs):**
- 3 steps. Each under 20 words. Setup should feel achievable in under 10 minutes.

**Social proof:**
- 1–2 quotes from users who use both tools (ideally job title + company size).

**FAQ (3 questions):** Appropriate to this integration — data flow direction, syncing frequency, permission scope.

Tone: Integration-confident, zero-friction framing. This reader is evaluating the tool by whether the integration is good, not whether the main product is worth trying.

### 4. Homepage Hero Section — PLG / Self-Serve SaaS

Write the above-the-fold hero section for a product-led growth SaaS homepage.

Target scenario: Organic + paid traffic, some brand awareness, self-serve onboarding (no sales call required to start). The visitor knows their problem; they're evaluating solutions.

Deliverables:

**H1** (8–14 words): Outcome-first. The visitor's goal, not the product category.
- Good: "Close deals faster with a CRM your team actually uses."
- Bad: "The leading AI-powered sales automation platform."

**Subheadline** (1–2 sentences, 25–40 words): Mechanism + audience + key differentiator.

**Proof strip** (3 short social proof signals, displayed in a row):
- Format options: Star rating + G2 ranking / Trustpilot score / Customer count / "Used by [X] teams at [companies]."

**CTA block:**
- Primary: "Start free" or "Try [Product] free — no credit card."
- Secondary: "See a 3-minute demo" (links to a short walkthrough video).

**Trust-building microcopy** (2 lines, below CTAs):
- "Free for [X seats/days/volume]."
- "[XX,000] teams signed up this month." (Social proof in microcopy.)

Tone: Confident, specific, product-literate visitor-first. Never "revolutionize" or "transform." Describe real work real users do.
