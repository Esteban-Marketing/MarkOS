# Marketing Services — Lifecycle & Email Industry Overlay Prompts

> **Context:** Marketing services email lifecycle is sales-cycle management for a high-consideration, high-trust purchase. CMO-level buyers receive many agency outreach emails; the ones that convert are segment-specific, proof-dense, and low-pressure. The sequence moves the prospect through: initial credibility (audit/asset delivery), proof accumulation (case study and methodology drip), and commitment removal (proposal and objection handling). Promotional or generic email performs poorly with marketing-expert buyers.

## Prompts

### 1. Inbound Lead Follow-Up and RFP Invitation Sequence

Write a 3-email sequence for a [marketing agency] following up on an inbound inquiry (audit request, contact form, or content download).

Email 1 — Delivery + first impression (send: immediately):
- Subject: "Your [audit / benchmark report / content] from [Agency Name]"
- Body: Deliver the promised asset or audit findings immediately; 2-sentence context on what's in it and how to use the findings; single next step: "If any of these findings match what you're experiencing, I'd be glad to walk through them — here's a 20-minute slot: [calendar link]"; no pitch

Email 2 — Relevant case study (send: 48 hours after Email 1, if no meeting booked):
- Subject: "How [anonymized: 'a company like yours'] solved [specific problem from their audit findings or content interest]"
- Body: 200-word case study matched to their stated challenge or the context of what they downloaded; specific metrics; single CTA: "Would this approach be a fit for [their company]? [Calendar link]"

Email 3 — RFP invitation or agency presentation offer (send: Day 5–7 if no meeting):
- Subject: "Would a brief on [their channel challenge] make sense?"
- Body: 3-line setup — acknowledge they're likely evaluating options; offer to send a capabilities brief matched to their specific situation (not a generic deck); make the ask explicit and low-commitment: "If you'd share the current challenge you're solving for, I'll send a relevant case study or brief. No pitch call required."

### 2. Proposal Follow-Up and Differentiation Sequence

Write a 3-email post-proposal sequence for a [marketing agency], supporting a CMO or marketing director who received a proposal and is now comparing options.

Email 1 — Proposal delivery confirmation + differentiation anchor (send: immediately after proposal):
- Subject: "Your [Agency Name] proposal — plus one thing most proposals don't show"
- Body: Confirm delivery; point to the one section of the proposal that differentiates the agency from typical pitches (methodology appendix, attribution model, or performance guarantee terms); offer a 30-minute "build vs. buy" conversation that is explicitly not a sales call — it's to help them make the right decision for their situation

Email 2 — Reference or additional proof (send: 3–5 days after proposal):
- Subject: "A case study you might not have seen — relevant to [their company type or challenge]"
- Body: Share a case study that directly parallels their situation (not generic proof — specific to what they're solving for); offer a customer reference call if they want to hear from a peer; no pressure, no deadline

Email 3 — Objection removal (send: Day 8–10 if no response):
- Subject: "The two questions we hear most at this stage"
- Body: Address the 2 most common CMO objections in writing — e.g., (1) "What happens if results take longer than projected?" → describe the performance review process and contract protection; (2) "What's the onboarding time before we see results?" → specific timeline from kickoff to first campaign live; close: "Happy to address anything else — no call needed if you prefer email."

### 3. Client Onboarding Sequence

Write a 3-email client onboarding sequence for a [marketing agency], starting when a contract is signed.

Email 1 — Contract signed + kickoff invitation (send: same day as contract execution):
- Subject: "Welcome to [Agency Name] — your kickoff is being scheduled"
- Body: Congratulate warmly but briefly; kickoff meeting invitation (date, attendees, agenda); what to prepare before kickoff (access grants, shared assets, account logins, team introductions); what will be accomplished in the first 30 days

Email 2 — Pre-kickoff preparation guide (send: 48 hours before kickoff):
- Subject: "Kickoff prep: what to have ready for [date]"
- Body: Checklist format — 5–7 specific items (account access, existing campaign data access, brand guidelines, competitor list); who needs to attend from client side (and why); time investment estimate for first 2 weeks; first 30-day deliverables preview

Email 3 — Post-kickoff and 30-day plan confirmation (send: 24 hours after kickoff):
- Subject: "Kickoff complete. Your 30-day plan is confirmed."
- Body: Recap of agreed priorities from kickoff; specific milestones for days 7, 14, and 30; communication cadence (weekly update format, Slack/Teams channel, reporting access); "If anything changes on your side before [Day 14], here's how to reach us."

### 4. Quarterly Business Review Preparation and Expansion Email

Write a 2-email QBR sequence for a [marketing agency], sent to the client CMO or marketing director 2–4 weeks before a quarterly business review.

Email 1 — QBR invitation + performance preview (send: 3–4 weeks before QBR):
- Subject: "[Client Company] Q[N] Performance Review — [date] — agenda preview"
- Body: QBR date confirmation; 3-line preview of key results to cover (specific KPIs — ROAS, CPL, pipeline contribution, revenue influenced); topic agenda: performance review, next quarter priorities, agency-client team alignment; request for any client-side agenda additions; note that one agenda item will cover potential scope expansion — will share options without pressure

Email 2 — Pre-QBR data brief (send: 2–3 days before QBR):
- Subject: "[Client Company] Q[N] snapshot — numbers before the conversation"
- Body: Summary table of Q[N] KPI actuals vs. targets vs. prior quarter; 1-sentence explanation for any metric that missed target and what's being adjusted; 2 highlights from the quarter (campaign or creative that overperformed); preview of Q[N+1] priority areas; CTA to confirm attendance or request a time change
