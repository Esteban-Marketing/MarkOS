# Professional Services — Social Industry Overlay Prompts

> **Context:** Professional services social content is practitioner-authored or practitioner-attributed — anonymous firm content performs poorly with senior buyers who are evaluating who they will work with, not just what the firm does. LinkedIn is the dominant channel. Content that performs in this vertical is either a genuine diagnostic insight from client work, a methodology observation with practitioner authority, or a peer signal (client milestone, referral, or colleague recognition). Volume-first content calendars do not fit this vertical — a single high-credibility post by a named partner outperforms ten firm-authored brand posts.

## Prompts

### 1. Practitioner Thought Leadership Post

Write a LinkedIn thought leadership post for a named [partner / principal / director] at a [management consulting / advisory / professional services firm].

Create 3 variants for different content angles:

**Variant A — Diagnostic framework from client work:**
- Hook: A specific observation from recent client engagements — "In the last [N] engagements where we were brought in after [trigger event], we saw the same pattern..."
- Observation: Describe the pattern in 2–3 sentences — specific enough to be credible, anonymised sufficiently to protect clients
- Framework: Present the 3-part (or 4-part) diagnostic lens the practitioner uses when they see this pattern; label each dimension with a memorable name
- Implication: 1 clear implication for [function] leaders who are reading this
- Byline close: "[Name] is a [title] at [Firm], specialising in [practice area]." — 1 sentence only; no self-promotional close
- Format: 250–350 words; no bullet lists in hook; framework section may use brief bullets

**Variant B — Client pattern observation:**
- Hook: A counter-intuitive observation about a common belief in the market — "Most [function] leaders I talk to believe [common assumption]. In my experience, it's usually the opposite."
- Evidence: 2–3 anonymised examples drawn from real engagement patterns
- Reframe: What the correct mental model is, and why the common assumption leads to the wrong decision
- Practitioner close: 1 personal note about why understanding this pattern changed how the practitioner approaches [specific part of their work]
- Format: 200–300 words; conversational but authoritative; no promotional content

**Variant C — Industry or regulatory development observation:**
- Hook: Reference a recent development (regulatory, market, structural) and immediately take a position — not a summary, a point of view
- Analysis: 3 implications of this development for [target function/sector]; be specific about what changes for practitioners in this role
- What to do: 2–3 concrete actions — not generic "wait and see" advice
- Format: 300–400 words; may include a brief list for the implications section; formal register

### 2. Conference, Speaking, or Publication Highlight

Write a LinkedIn post series for a [professional services practitioner] who is speaking at a conference, publishing an article, or participating in a panel on [topic].

Create 3 variants for different moments in the event cycle:

**Variant A — Pre-event announcement:**
- Context: [Name] is speaking at [Event] on [Date] on [topic]
- Hook: The most provocative or counter-intuitive point from the talk — give away a real insight, not just a tease
- Why this talk: 1–2 sentences on why this topic matters now, from the practitioner's perspective
- Invitation: "If you're attending [Event], I'd value the conversation. [Link to session or DM offer]"
- Format: 150–200 words; warm and direct; not a marketing announcement

**Variant B — Post-event insight share:**
- Context: [Name] just presented / participated in [Event]
- Leading insight: The one thing that surprised them in the room — audience reaction, a question they hadn't anticipated, a point from another speaker that challenged their view
- Takeaway for those not there: Translate the session insight into a practical observation for [function] leaders who weren't present — "If you didn't attend, here's what matters for you..."
- Format: 200–250 words; reflective and genuine; avoid summary-for-summary's-sake

**Variant C — Publication or article launch:**
- Context: [Name] has published [article title / methodology piece / guide] in [publication or via firm]
- Hook: The core argument of the piece in 2 sentences — the most challenging or interesting claim
- Why write it: 1 sentence on what prompted the piece — a pattern observed, a gap in existing thinking
- Link: [Link to article]
- Format: 100–150 words; brief and direct; the link does the work

### 3. Client Engagement Milestone

Write a LinkedIn post series for a [professional services firm] marking a completed engagement, a methodology milestone, or a team recognition moment.

Create 3 variants:

**Variant A — Engagement completion (anonymised):**
- Context: A major engagement has concluded; client name anonymised as [industry descriptor + company type]
- Lead: The outcome — specific metric or transformation, not generic "successful engagement"
- Context: What the challenge was, what made this engagement complex or interesting from a practitioner perspective
- Recognition: Acknowledge the client team's contribution to the outcome — by role or function, not name; and the internal team composition
- Reflection: 1 sentence from the lead practitioner on what they learned from this specific engagement
- No CTA unless a follow-on invitation is genuinely appropriate (e.g., hosting a webinar on the methodology used)

**Variant B — Methodology milestone (e.g., published framework, 50th engagement using a named approach):**
- Lead: Name the milestone — "We have now used [Framework Name] in [N] client engagements across [N] sectors"
- What the framework does: 1–2 sentences; link to a methodology page or article
- What the milestone means: What the firm has learned from [N] applications that they didn't know at the start; a specific refinement or evolution of the approach
- Invitation: Offer a relevant resource (methodology paper, briefing) for readers who work in [practice area]

**Variant C — Team recognition (promotion, certification, publication):**
- Context: A named team member has been promoted, certified, or published
- Lead: The individual's name, new title or credential, and what it signals about their practice depth
- Substance: 2–3 sentences about why this person's credentialing matters for clients — what expertise they now bring; avoid generic congratulations posts
- Connect: Offer a way for readers to connect with this practitioner on the topic they specialise in

### 4. Practitioner Credential and Promotion Announcement

Write a LinkedIn announcement post series for a [professional services firm] recognising practitioner achievements: a partner promotion, a new certification, or a named methodology publication.

Create 3 variants:

**Variant A — Partner / director promotion announcement:**
- Lead: "[Name] has been elected [Partner / promoted to Director] at [Firm], effective [date]."
- Credential context: Not career history — focus on the specific practice expertise [Name] brings and why it matters for clients; 2–3 sentences on their practitioner depth in [area]
- What they work on: 1 sentence on the types of engagements [Name] leads and the client profiles they typically support
- Invitation: For readers who work in [Name]'s practice area, offer an introduction or relevant content — not a generic "reach out any time"

**Variant B — Certification or accreditation:**
- Lead: "[Name] has achieved [specific certification/accreditation] — [brief name of the body or governing organisation]."
- Why this credential matters: What the accreditation represents in practice — what a practitioner must demonstrate to achieve it; why it is relevant to the work [Firm] does with clients
- Practical implication: How this strengthens the firm's delivery capability for [specific client type or challenge] — be specific
- Avoid: Generic "we're proud of" language

**Variant C — Named methodology or practitioner publication:**
- Lead: "[Name] has published [title] — [1 sentence on what it contributes to [practice area] thinking]"
- Core argument: The 1 most important claim or framework from the publication
- Who it's for: Specific in audience — "[Function] leaders navigating [specific challenge]" not "anyone interested in [broad topic]"
- Access: Link to the publication or offer to share it with relevant connections
