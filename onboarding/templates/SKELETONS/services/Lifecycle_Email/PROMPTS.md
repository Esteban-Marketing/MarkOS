# Services — Lifecycle Email Starter Prompts

> **Context:** Email in services businesses maintains relationships across a long sales cycle that may take weeks or months to close. Unlike ecommerce, email here never pushes to purchase — it builds the conditions for trust, demonstrates ongoing value, and keeps the firm top-of-mind until the prospect is ready to engage. Prompts here cover lead nurture content sequences, proposal follow-up, no-response re-engagement, and post-close onboarding.

## Prompts

### 1. Lead Nurture Sequence — Content-Led (3 Emails Over 2–3 Weeks)

Write a 3-email lead nurture sequence for a professional services firm nurturing a prospect who downloaded or engaged with content (not yet a proposal conversation).

Context: Firm practice area: [e.g., "supply chain consulting", "executive coaching"]. Prospect context: [ICP job title, company type]. Nurture goal: Move from "aware of the firm" to "considering a conversation."

**Email 1 — Relevant Insight (Send at day 0–1 after opt-in/content engagement):**
- Subject: Insight-led, not pitch-led. Example: "The [ICP] problem we hear most"
- Body (120–150 words): Relevant expertise observation. Something specific to their role or industry. No pitch. 1 link to a relevant article or case study.
- Sign-off: From a named practitioner, not "The [Firm] Team."

**Email 2 — Case Proof (Send at day 7):**
- Subject: "[Company type] case: how they resolved [problem]"
- Body (150–180 words): Brief case study format — client context, challenge, approach, outcome. "We're seeing this pattern often in [industry] — happy to send the full case study if relevant."
- CTA: "Reply to this email and I'll send the full version." (Low commitment — reply-based, not a form.)

**Email 3 — Soft Ask (Send at day 14–21):**
- Subject: "Worth a conversation?"
- Body (80–100 words): Direct and warm. "We've shared a few things since [event]. If any of it resonates with what you're working through, a 30-minute call might be worth the time. No agenda — just a look at your situation." Discovery call link.

Tone across sequence: Practitioner-to-practitioner. Informative, non-pushy. Each email should provide value whether or not the prospect converts.

### 2. Proposal Follow-Up Sequence — After Sending a Proposal

Write a post-proposal follow-up email sequence for a professional services engagement (for prospects who have received a proposal but haven't responded).

Context: Engagement type: [scope of services proposed]. Proposal sent date: [X]. Typical decision timeline for this engagement type: [X weeks].

**Email 1 — Gentle Check-In (3–5 days after proposal sent):**
- Subject: "Following up on the proposal — any questions?"
- Body (80–100 words): Thank them for the time to present. Ask if they have questions on any part of the scope or pricing. State you're available for a quick call if it would help.
- Tone: Service-stance, not pressure. "Happy to clarify anything."

**Email 2 — Relevant Context (10–14 days after proposal sent — if no response):**
- Subject: Add relevant supporting asset — "[Practice area] case study that might be relevant"
- Body (100–120 words): Acknowledge you're checking in again. Share one relevant piece of supporting information (case study, methodology note, a relevant observation). "Not a nudge — just something I thought might be useful context."
- Tone: Adds value rather than applies pressure.

**Email 3 — Close/Release (21–28 days after proposal sent):**
- Subject: "Should we pause on this?"
- Body (60–80 words): Direct, gracious, no pressure. "We haven't connected since sending the proposal. Happy to revisit when the timing is better, or if you have questions in the future. Let me know if you'd like us to pause for now."
- Tone: Respectful of their time and situation. Leaves the door open without any pressure signal.

### 3. Client Onboarding Email — Week One Communication

Write a post-close client onboarding email sequence (2 emails) for a professional services engagement.

Context: Engagement type: [service type and scope]. Kickoff date: [date or relative]. Primary point of contact at the firm: [named role]. Primary stakeholder at client: [named role/title].

**Email 1 — Post-Signature / Welcome (day of signing or next day):**
- Subject: "Welcome — what happens next with [Firm Name]"
- Body (150–180 words):
  - Warm acknowledgment of the commitment. One sentence.
  - What the first 2 weeks look like, specifically: kickoff call, what to prepare, who to involve.
  - Who at the firm owns what: Point of contact name, availability, how to reach them outside scheduled calls.
  - One reassurance signal: "If anything comes up before kickoff, [name] is available at [contact]."
  - Sign-off: From the named engagement lead, not "The Team."

**Email 2 — Pre-Kickoff Prep (2–3 days before kickoff):**
- Subject: "Kickoff on [date] — here's what to bring"
- Body (120–150 words): Short and specific. What to prepare for the kickoff (data, stakeholders to include, decisions to have made in advance). What the kickoff will produce by the end. "This call sets the foundation for [phase 1 outcome]."
- Tone: Organized confidence. This email makes the client feel they made a sophisticated choice.

### 4. Re-Engagement Email — Lapsed Lead or Inactive Interest

Write a re-engagement email for a professional services firm reaching out to a prospect who showed interest but went inactive (no response to previous outreach or lapsed from a warm conversation).

Context: Last contact: [X months ago]. Original context: [How they engaged — attended webinar, downloaded content, had an intro call]. Relevant update since then: [New case study, new service, or timely market development].

**Email (single email, 100–120 words):**
- Subject: Period-aware, not generic. Examples: "Checking in — 6 months on" / "Something relevant to your Q4 situation" / "[Name], a new case study you might find useful."
- Body:
  - Brief recall: How and when you last connected.
  - Relevant new value: What's new or different that's relevant to their situation.
  - Low-stakes offer: "Happy to share the full picture — no obligation." or "Worth a 20-minute call if it's relevant?"
- Tone: Warm re-introduction with a genuine reason to reconnect. Never: "I just wanted to circle back."
