# Services — Social Media Starter Prompts

> **Context:** LinkedIn is the dominant social channel for professional services. Content here builds practitioner authority through perspective, demonstrates proof through client results, and maintains top-of-mind presence with a well-defined ICP. Tone is peer-to-peer, never sales-first. Prompts here cover thought leadership POV posts, client results formats, process/methodology transparency posts, and event/talk content.

## Prompts

### 1. LinkedIn POV Post — Expert Take / Contrarian Perspective

Write a LinkedIn post sharing an expert point of view on a topic relevant to the firm's ICP and practice area.

Context: Practice area or expertise: [e.g., "pricing strategy consulting", "organizational change"]. Specific topic or tension: [The specific angle — a misconception, uncomfortable truth, or underappreciated dynamic]. ICP: [Job title and context].

Post structure (150–220 words):

**Opening hook (2 lines, no intro paragraph):**
- Start with the claim, not the context. Contrarian, surprising, or counterintuitive.
- Good: "Most [ICP] get [problem] backwards. Here's what actually matters."
- Bad: "I've been in [industry] for 15 years and I've learned a lot about..."

**Body (3–4 short paragraphs):**
- Build the argument: the conventional view, what it misses, the better framing.
- Include: One specific pattern observed from real engagements (anonymized).
- Avoid: Theory without evidence. Every claim should be grounded in the firm's observed practice.

**Closing (1–2 lines):**
- The practical implication for the reader. What should they reconsider or do differently?
- Optional: "Happy to share how we approach this — feel free to reach out."

Tone: Confident and earned. This is a practitioner who has observed a pattern — not a think-tank paper. No jargon, no hedging.

### 2. Client Result Post — Outcome + Process Fragment

Write a LinkedIn post sharing a client result from a professional services engagement.

Format:
- **Opening (outcome-first, 1–2 lines):** Lead with the specific, quantified result. Named client preferred; anonymized acceptable with industry context. "One of our clients in [industry] just wrapped [type of engagement]. [Specific outcome metric]."
- **Context (2 sentences):** What they were dealing with beforehand. Why conventional approaches hadn't resolved it.
- **Approach fragment (2–3 sentences):** What the engagement focused on. The distinctive element of the methodology that drove the result. Not a full case study — just enough for a competent peer to understand the mechanism.
- **Lesson or perspective (1 paragraph):** What this result suggests about the underlying problem more broadly. This is where the thought leadership lives.
- **Optional close:** "If you're dealing with something similar, happy to exchange notes."

Length: 180–230 words.
Tone: Practitioner-to-practitioner. Shares a real win without any sales energy. No "we're thrilled" or "we're proud." The result is the statement.

### 3. Process / "Behind the Work" Post — Methodology Transparency

Write a LinkedIn post that makes a piece of the firm's methodology or process visible to prospects.

Context: Process element: [Specific phase, activity, or decision framework the firm uses]. Why it matters: [What problem it solves and why many practitioners miss it].

Post structure:

**Opening:** Name the process element and why most engagements skip or underinvest in it.

**The case for doing it right:** 1 paragraph — what changes when this is done well vs. done poorly.

**How we approach it (3–4 bullet points or short paragraphs):**
- Specific steps or principles in the firm's approach.
- Enough detail for a sophisticated prospect to evaluate it.

**What this means for clients:**
- The practical outcome the client experiences because of this approach.

**Closing:** "If you're selecting a [type of firm/provider] for [practice area], this is a useful question to ask them about their process."

Length: 200–250 words.
Tone: Process-confident, generous with specifics. Teaching earns trust; vague claims don't. This post positions the firm as the one that has actually thought this through.

### 4. Repurposed Content Distribution — Article or Case Study Teaser

Write a LinkedIn post distributing a new article, case study, or methodology guide published by the firm.

Context: Content title: [Title]. What it covers: [Brief summary]. Target reader: [ICP].

Post structure (for maximum content distribution without CTR-baiting):

**Hook (1–2 lines):** The most counterintuitive or surprising claim in the piece.

**Value preview (3–5 bullets or short sentences):**
- What the piece covers (specific enough to earn the click for the right audience).
- Each bullet: One distinct insight or finding.

**Who it's useful for (1 sentence):**
- "This is most relevant for [ICP role and context]."

**CTA:** "[Read the full [article/case study/guide] here → [link]]"

**Optional comment prompt:** "We've been testing this with clients for [X] months — happy to share how it's landed in practice. Questions welcome."

Length: 120–160 words for the post body.
Tone: Curator's voice, not marketer's voice. The goal is to help the right person decide if this content is worth 10 minutes of their time.
