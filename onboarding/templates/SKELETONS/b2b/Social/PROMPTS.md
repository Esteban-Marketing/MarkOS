# B2B — Social Media Starter Prompts

> **Context:** B2B social is 90% LinkedIn. The content types that generate pipeline are: practitioner POV posts (thought leadership), case study fragments, and educational posts that demonstrate expertise without pitching. Posts authored by named individuals consistently outperform brand account posts in reach and trust. Prompts here are built for named-author LinkedIn content, not generic brand posts.

## Prompts

### 1. LinkedIn POV Post — Contrarian Take on a Common Practice

Write a 250–350 word LinkedIn post authored by [practitioner name/title, e.g., "a VP of Marketing at a B2B SaaS company"] sharing a contrarian view on a common practice in their space.

Format:
- **Hook (line 1)**: A bold, specific claim — the contrarian thesis in one sentence. No "I've been thinking about…" warm-up.
- **Setup (2–3 sentences)**: What the conventional wisdom says and why most people follow it.
- **The turn (3–4 sentences)**: Why it's wrong, incomplete, or misapplied — with a specific real-world example or outcome the author observed.
- **Evidence (2 sentences)**: One data point or named example that supports the argument.
- **Takeaway (2–3 sentences)**: What the audience should do or think differently.
- **Engagement closer**: End with a question that invites disagreement ("Am I wrong here? What's your experience?")

Tone: Direct, named perspective, practitioner credibility. No corporate brand voice. First person.

---

### 2. LinkedIn Case Study Fragment — One Outcome, One Quote

Write a LinkedIn post (150–200 words) that surfaces a single result from a customer success story, formatted for maximum credibility without a link or paywall.

Structure:
- **Opening stat**: Lead with the outcome metric (e.g., "One of our customers reduced their DSO by 34% in 90 days.")
- **One-sentence context**: What company/team type, what problem they started with
- **The mechanism (2 sentences)**: What specifically changed — not "they used our product," but what workflow, behavior, or decision changed
- **Customer quote**: One direct quote that names a specific outcome, not a vague endorsement ("This cut our month-end close from 5 days to 2.")
- **Closing**: One sentence pointing toward the full case study or a relevant CTA (optional)

Tone: Outcome-first, specific, not a marketing newsletter. Feel like insider knowledge, not a press release.

---

### 3. LinkedIn Educational Post — "How We Do X" Transparency

Write a 200–300 word LinkedIn post authored by [practitioner name] sharing an internal process, framework, or lesson learned in the author's area of expertise.

Format:
- **Frame the problem (2 sentences)**: What challenge or question prompted the process
- **The process (3–5 bullets)**: The specific steps, decisions, or principles — named and concrete, not generic best practices
- **What didn't work first (1–2 sentences)**: An honest moment of failure or iteration — builds trust
- **What changed (1–2 sentences)**: The actual improvement or result
- **Takeaway or question**: What you'd tell someone starting from scratch, or a question for the audience

Tone: Practitioner transparency. This is the opposite of "our product solves this" content. The product is the author's expertise, not the vendor's tool.

---

### 4. LinkedIn Employee Advocacy Post — Celebrating a Customer Win

Write a LinkedIn post for a B2B company employee (any level) celebrating a customer milestone or win. Goal: authentic signal of the company's real-world impact without sounding like a press release.

Requirements:
- Name the customer or describe them specifically (with permission, or use anonymized-but-specific: "a 400-person logistics company in the midwest")
- Name the outcome: what changed, what they gained, or what they can do now that they couldn't before
- One personal line from the employee: what this win means to them or what they're proud of
- Optional: tag the customer contact (if they've given permission)
- End: a genuine observation about why this matters, not a product pitch

Length: 100–150 words
Tone: Proud but grounded, colleague-like, genuinely celebratory without corporate enthusiasm.
