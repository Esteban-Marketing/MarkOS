# B2B — Lifecycle Email Starter Prompts

> **Context:** B2B lifecycle email is sales-aligned and persona-aware. The highest-performing sequences are behavior-triggered (not blasted), timed to the buyer's stage in the cycle, and written with the awareness that multiple stakeholders may be forwarding or reviewing the same thread. Prompts here cover: lead nurture post-content download, trial-to-sales-qualified handoff, and renewal expansion sequences.

## Prompts

### 1. Lead Nurture Sequence — Post-Content Download (3 Emails)

Write a 3-email nurture sequence for a B2B SaaS company. Triggered when a lead downloads a gated asset (e.g., research report or buyer's guide). Goal: build trust and surface sales intent over 7–10 days.

**Email 1 (Day 0 — Delivery + Value Add):**
- Subject: Deliver the asset with one additional resource the lead didn't ask for (a related article, a customer story, or a quick-start checklist)
- CTA: Soft — "Let us know if you have questions" or link to related content
- Length: 100–120 words
- Tone: helpful, non-pushy, colleague-like

**Email 2 (Day 3 — Mechanism + Social Proof):**
- Subject: Address the core pain from the downloaded asset in a new angle
- Body: Share a 2–3 sentence customer story that names the outcome (not a testimonial page link — the story itself, in the email)
- CTA: "We put together a 5-minute walkthrough if you want to see how [company] solves this — no commitment"
- Length: 120–150 words

**Email 3 (Day 7 — Intent Signal):**
- Subject: Light ask — schedule a 20-minute call or start a free trial
- Body: Acknowledge they've been thinking about this (without being creepy about tracking). Name what the call / trial will accomplish — specific outcome, not "learn more."
- CTA: Book a call link or trial CTA
- Length: 80–100 words

### 2. Trial-to-Demo Qualification Email — Sales Handoff

Write an email sent by a sales rep to a B2B trial user who has been active in the product for 5–7 days but hasn't booked a demo or reached the key activation moment.

Goal: Transition from self-serve trial to a qualified sales conversation without feeling like a cold pitch.

Requirements:
- From: Named sales rep (not "the team")
- Subject: Reference the trial activity specifically without being surveillance-y (e.g., "Quick question about your [Product] setup")
- Body (100–130 words): Acknowledge they've been in the product. Ask one specific question about what they're trying to accomplish. Offer to help them get there faster — not to "show them features."
- CTA: Calendar link for a 20-min "get you set up" call — framed as efficiency, not sales
- Tone: Helpful peer, not SDR following a script. No "I noticed you've visited our pricing page 3 times" energy.

### 3. Renewal + Expansion Email — 60 Days Before Contract End

Write an email from a Customer Success Manager to a B2B customer 60 days before their annual renewal.

Context: The account has been live for 10 months. Usage is moderate but not exceptional. Goal: anchor the renewal decision in outcomes, not contract language, and surface a natural expansion opportunity.

Requirements:
- Subject: Not "Your renewal is coming up" — frame it as a value checkpoint
- Opening (50 words): Acknowledge a specific win or outcome from the past year — name something real (metric or feature adoption) rather than generic appreciation
- Middle (80 words): Tee up the expansion opportunity as a natural next step based on what they've built — not a pitch for an add-on
- Close (40 words): Easy path to renewal + who to contact with questions
- Tone: Partner-level, not vendor. The email should feel like it was written by someone who knows this account.

---

### 4. Re-Engagement Email — Cold MQL (90 Days Inactive)

Write a B2B re-engagement email for a lead who downloaded content 90 days ago and has since gone dark. No trial, no demo booked, no recent email opens.

Goal: Restart the conversation without it feeling desperate.

Requirements:
- Subject: Pattern-break from typical nurture email (use a question or acknowledge the gap directly)
- Body (60–80 words): Don't recap everything they missed. Ask one question relevant to where they were in their decision process. Offer something genuinely useful (new content, a customer story in their industry) — not a discount.
- CTA: One link or one ask — not three options
- Unsubscribe-permission option: A polite "If the timing isn't right, no worries — I'll remove you from future emails" line. Paradoxically increases re-engagement.
- Tone: Honest, low-pressure, peer-like.
