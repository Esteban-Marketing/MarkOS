    # Professional Services — Paid Media Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Paid media for professional services supports a relationship-driven sales process — it does not replace it. The role of paid is to build name recognition with the right buyers before the RFP, reinforce credibility with prospects in active consideration, and amplify referral surface by keeping the firm visible to past clients and their networks. Direct-response paid rarely converts professional services clients; authority-building paid content earns the right to be on the shortlist.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. LinkedIn Campaign Targeting C-Suite and VP-Level Decision-Makers

    Write a LinkedIn paid campaign brief and ad creative for a [consulting / advisory firm] targeting C-suite (CEO, CFO, COO, CHRO) and VP-level decision-makers at [target company size / industry].

    Campaign structure:
    - **Audience targeting:** Job title list (CEO, CFO, COO, CHRO, VP Strategy, VP Operations, Managing Director) + company size (100–10,000 employees depending on ICP) + industry filter (if vertical-specialist firm); consider excluding current clients to avoid cannibalization
    - **Campaign objective:** Website conversions (white paper / report download) or brand awareness (video views) — NOT lead gen form for cold outreach at this stage; professional services buyers resist premature lead capture
    - **Ad format:** Single image or document ad for a named thought leadership piece (research report, framework overview, or point of view paper)
    - **Ad creative (Document Ad or Sponsored Content):**
      - Document/content title visible in ad: "[Framework or Report Name] — [What It Helps Leaders Decide or Do]"
      - Intro text (600 chars): Lead with a specific boardroom or C-suite challenge — not a services pitch; establish the firm's perspective in 2 sentences; offer the document as a useful resource, not a lead magnet: "We produced this for [client type] leaders working through [challenge]. No form. Direct download."
      - Removing the form creates higher-quality brand impressions; use retargeting (Prompt 2) for the conversion step once content has been consumed
    - **Retargeting setup:** Build a retargeting audience of content downloaders and content engagers within 30 days; use this audience for Prompt 2 (thought leadership retargeting with consultation offer)

    ### 2. Google Retargeting with Thought Leadership and Methodology Proof Content

    Write a Google Display and YouTube retargeting campaign for a [consulting / advisory firm] targeting buyers who have visited the website but not made contact.

    - **Segment B — Visited team or credentials page (consideration):** Message: Client outcome proof — "[Anonymized client type]'s [specific challenge] resolved with [framework name]. Read how." CTA: "Request a brief."
    - **Segment C — Visited contact or request page, no submission (high intent):** Message: Remove friction — "Thinking about starting an engagement? No RFP required for an initial scoping conversation." CTA: "Schedule 30 minutes with [named practitioner]." Direct calendar link to a named partner or principal builds more trust than a generic contact form.

    For each segment: write Google Display ad (headline 30 chars + description 90 chars + call to action text).

    - **Search keywords targeting evaluation-stage queries:** "consulting firm for [specialty]," "[specialty] advisory firm," "specialist vs. big four consulting," "[specialty] consulting [city/region]," "[client challenge] consulting"

    # Professional Services — Content & SEO Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Professional services SEO is driven by expertise-signal queries — buyers searching for authoritative perspectives on their specific challenge, not just "consulting firm" category searches. The highest-converting content makes the practitioner's thinking visible: published methodologies, original research on industry trends, and case studies that show how the firm's approach leads to specific outcomes. Content that answers "how do I evaluate a firm like this?" directly captures late-funnel buyers who are already in evaluation mode.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. Practice Area / Methodology Overview Page (SEO)

    Write an SEO-optimized practice area overview page for a [consulting / advisory firm] targeting the keyword "[practice area] consulting" or "[challenge type] advisory."

    Page structure:
    - **H1:** "[Practice Area] Advisory and Consulting: [Outcome-Led Positioning for Target Client Type]" — e.g., "Organizational Transformation Advisory: Helping Mid-Market Companies Navigate Structural Change"
    - **Intro (100 words):** Establish the problem this practice area addresses — in the language of the buyer who has this problem; state the firm's perspective and approach in 2 sentences; establish the depth of the practice (years, engagements, named methodology if applicable)
    - **Methodology overview (200 words):** Name the firm's approach — don't use generic consulting terminology; describe the phases or steps and what makes each phase distinctive; what the client team's experience is like during the engagement
    - **Practitioner credentials section:** 2–3 lead practitioners for this practice area — name, specific background, years in domain, named certifications or frameworks used; professional photo placeholder note; link to individual bio page
    - **Client outcomes (3 case examples, anonymized):** Each: client profile, presenting challenge, approach used, specific outcome; calibrate to be specific enough that a similar-profile client self-identifies
    - **"Is this engagement right for you?" section (100 words):** Explicit — who benefits most from this practice; minimum engagement profile (size, complexity, readiness); and honestly, what type of client is a poor fit
    - **CTA:** "Request a scoping conversation with our [practice area] lead" — named practitioner with direct contact or calendar link

    ---

    ### 2. Industry Trend or Regulatory Update Content (Credentialing)

    Write a thought leadership article or research summary for a [consulting / advisory firm] on a current industry trend, regulatory change, or emerging challenge relevant to their target client base.

    Content structure:
    - **H1:** "[Trend/Regulation/Challenge]: What [Client Type] Need to Know — and Do — in [Year]"
    - **Intro (100 words):** Frame the trend as a genuine business challenge, not an opportunity for the firm — perspective-first, not pitch-first; establish the practitioner voice ("From our work with [N] [client type] organizations in the past 18 months, we've observed...")
    - **What's actually changing (200 words):** The substance of the trend or regulatory change — specific, named entities, dates, or thresholds; correct any common misconceptions about the change
    - **What most [client type] are getting wrong (150 words):** 3 specific misunderstandings or common response patterns that are ineffective; cite evidence where possible (client patterns, industry data, practitioner observation)
    - **The recommended response framework (200 words):** Named approach with 3–4 steps; calibrated to the readiness and resource level of the target client; not a sales pitch for a comprehensive engagement — must be genuinely useful as a standalone resource
    - **What this means for your roadmap (100 words):** Timing, urgency, and prioritization guidance — when to act, what to do first
    - **CTA:** "Speak with our [practice area] lead about your organization's readiness" + link to practice area page; secondary: "Download our [related framework / regulatory readiness checklist]" (gated or ungated)

    ---

    ### 3. Client Success Case Study (SEO Format)

    Write a case study in SEO content format for a [consulting / advisory firm], targeting searches for "[practice area] case study" or "[challenge type] consulting results."

    Case study structure:
    - **H1:** "How [anonymized profile: 'a 1,200-person financial services firm'] Resolved [Specific Challenge] with [Practice Area / Methodology]"
    - **Client context (100 words):** Organization profile — industry, size, structure, and the specific presenting challenge; enough detail that a similar-profile reader immediately recognizes their situation; do not name the client unless explicit permission granted
    - **Why they engaged [Firm] (80 words):** What the client was looking for that they had not found elsewhere; what differentiated the firm in the selection process — be specific about the credential, methodology, or team factor cited
    - **Engagement approach (200 words):** What the firm did, in what order, and why; name the methodology or framework used; describe the client team's involvement; note the timeline and key milestones
    - **Outcomes (100 words):** Primary outcome with specific, attributable metric; secondary outcomes (3 additional); timeline from engagement start to measurable result; honest note on what took longer than expected if applicable (builds credibility)
    - **What the client says:** Named or anonymized quote from sponsor — specific to the methodology and outcome, not generic praise
    - **Related content:** Link to practice area page; link to related trend/regulatory article; "Similar engagements" section with 2 related case study links
    - **CTA:** "Tell us about a similar challenge" → scoping conversation form

    ---

    ### 4. "How to Evaluate a Professional Services Firm" Buyer's Guide

    Write an SEO buyer's guide for a [consulting / advisory firm] targeting prospects who are in active evaluation mode — comparing firms before making a selection or issuing an RFP.

    Guide structure:
    - **H1:** "How to Select a [Practice Area] Consulting Firm: A Practical Evaluation Guide for [Buyer Role, e.g., 'CFOs and Strategy Teams']"
    - **Intro (100 words):** Frame this as a neutral resource — the goal is to help the buyer make a good selection, not to lead them toward this firm; establish the author's perspective (we've been on the shortlist many times; here's what we've observed separates good decisions from poor ones)
    - **Evaluation criteria framework (5–7 criteria, 80 words each):**
      - Practitioner credentials: What to verify — certifications, named methodology, practitioner vs. firm background
      - Domain depth vs. generalist breadth: When each matters and how to assess it
      - Principal-level access: How to evaluate whether senior practitioners lead the work or transfer it to junior staff after kickoff
      - Reference depth: Not just "can you provide references" but what to ask them (questions provided)
      - Engagement model and risk posture: Fixed scope, time-and-materials, or outcome-linked; what each signals about the firm's confidence in their approach
      - Cultural and working style fit: Why this matters more than it sounds for 6–18 month engagements
    - **RFP structure guidance (100 words):** How to write an RFP that surfaces genuine differentiation rather than who has the best proposal writer; 5 specific questions to include that expose approach and capability
    - **Red flags section (100 words):** 5 evaluation red flags — behaviors at proposal or pitch stage that predict poor engagement experience
    - **CTA:** "See how [Firm] addresses each of these criteria" → link to practice area page or team/credentials page

    # Professional Services — Lifecycle & Email Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Professional services email lifecycle mirrors the relationship-led sales cycle: slow to start, high trust required, and highly referral-dependent for re-engagement. The most effective sequences are low-frequency, high-quality, and explicitly non-promotional — RFP and proposal processes require formal, structured communication that matches the buyer's professional register. Post-engagement referral activation is the single highest-ROI email use case in this vertical.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. RFP Inquiry Acknowledgment, Qualification, and Social Proof Sequence

    Write a 3-email sequence for a [consulting / advisory firm] responding to an inbound RFP inquiry or project inquiry submission.

    Email 1 — Inquiry acknowledgment + qualification (send: within 4 business hours):
    - Subject: "Re: [Project topic or inquiry subject] — [Firm Name]"
    - Body: Acknowledge receipt; confirm who will be the point of contact (named, with title); state the timeline for response (we will send a preliminary response or scoping questions within [X] business days); ask 3–4 qualifying questions to understand fit before investing proposal time — frame these as "to make sure we address your situation accurately," not as screening; close with a brief credentialing signal: one relevant engagement or named expert the inquiry will be routed to

    Email 2 — Credentials and proof transmission (send: within 1–2 business days):
    - Subject: "Context on [Firm Name]'s work in [relevant practice area / client type]"
    - Body: Not a capabilities deck — a focused 3-paragraph brief: (1) Who handles this type of engagement at the firm (named principal or practice lead with specific credential); (2) 2 analogous case examples: client type, challenge, outcome metric; (3) What distinguishes the firm's approach for this type of work — be specific, not generic; CTA: "I'll follow up with scoping questions / a preliminary proposal by [date]"

    Email 3 — Scoping questions or preliminary proposal offer (send: within agreed timeline):
    - Subject: "Scoping questions for [Project topic] / Preliminary approach for [inquiry subject]"
    - Body: If scoping questions: present 5–7 focused questions that will determine scope, timeline, and approach — frame them around what you need to be useful, not what you need to produce a proposal; if preliminary approach: brief outline of recommended approach, fee range, and timeline — make clear this is subject to scoping, not a binding proposal; CTA: "A 45-minute scoping conversation would help us give you a more useful proposal. [Calendar link to named principal]"
