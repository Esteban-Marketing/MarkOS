    # Travel & Hospitality — Paid Media Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Travel paid media converts best when it closes the aspiration-to-booking gap. The buyer has emotional intent; the ad's job is to reduce friction (price anxiety, availability doubt, comparison paralysis) and make the booking action feel obvious. Destination intent keywords, seasonal peak bidding windows, and review-anchored retargeting are the highest-ROI levers in this vertical.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. Destination Intent Search Campaign

    Write a Google Search ad campaign for a [hotel / resort / tour operator / OTA] targeting destination planning keywords for [destination name or region].

    Campaign parameters:
    - **Target keywords (examples):** "[destination] hotels," "best places to stay in [destination]," "[destination] [month/season] travel," "[property type] [destination]"
    - **Match types:** Exact and phrase match for high-intent; broad match modified for discovery
    - **Ad copy (3 RSA headlines, max 30 chars each):** Lead headline with destination name + property type; second headline with strongest proof hook (star rating, #1 ranking, number of guests/year); third headline with urgency or offer (book direct, best rate guarantee)
    - **Ad copy (2 descriptions, max 90 chars each):** First description: sensory destination hook + standout property feature; second description: social proof stat + booking friction reducer (free cancellation, instant confirmation)
    - **Extensions:** Sitelinks to top room types, dining, spa, and "Best Rate Guarantee" page; callout extensions for check-in/check-out flexibility, free breakfast, beachfront/poolside

    Tone: Aspirational but grounded. The buyer is in planning mode — match that intent; don't oversell.

    ---

    ### 2. Seasonal Peak Bidding Strategy

    Write a paid media campaign brief and ad copy framework for a [property/operator] preparing for [high-demand season: summer, school holidays, festive period, cherry blossom, ski season].

    Brief structure:
    - **Budget rationale:** Why bid costs increase in this window and how to model CPL/CPA at +30–50% CPCs
    - **Audience layering:** How to stack first-party (past bookers, email list) + in-market (Google/Meta travel intent) audiences for efficiency
    - **Ad copy variants (3 headlines × 2 descriptions for Google RSA; 1 primary + 1 secondary for Meta):**
      - Variant A: Urgency-first — availability as the scarcity signal ("Only 4 rooms left for [peak dates]")
      - Variant B: Value-first — what the season makes special about this destination/property
      - Variant C: Social proof — number of guests who stayed last [same season] or review score for the season
    - **Landing page alignment:** What the landing page must show to match seasonal ad intent (date picker with peak availability, seasonal imagery, seasonal package pricing)

    Include a note on bid strategy: manual CPC vs Target CPA/ROAS for peak vs shoulder periods.

    ---

    ### 3. Retargeting: Warm Audiences with Last-Minute Urgency

    Write a retargeting ad campaign for [hotel/resort/operator] targeting users who visited the booking page or viewed specific room/package pages but did not complete a reservation.

    Audience segments and message by segment:
    - **Segment A — Viewed property page, did not search dates (awareness-level drop-off):** Message: destination aspiration + "where will you go next?" soft re-engagement
    - **Segment B — Searched dates, did not select a room (consideration drop-off):** Message: specific room category they viewed + availability urgency + review proof hook
    - **Segment C — Added to cart / reached payment page (high-intent drop-off):** Message: "Still thinking about [destination]? [N] rooms left for your dates." Direct CTA: Complete booking. Include price guarantee or free cancellation if applicable.

    For each segment write: 2 Meta ad copy variants (primary text + headline) and 1 Google Display copy variant (headline + description).

    Tone: Warm, not pushy. Last-minute urgency must be real (actual availability) — never manufactured scarcity.

    ---

    ### 4. Meta-Search and OTA Competitive Bidding

    Write a paid search campaign strategy and ad copy set for a [hotel/resort] targeting branded and competitive keywords where OTAs (Booking.com, Expedia, Hotels.com) are bidding.

    Strategy sections:
    - **Own brand defense:** Why bidding on your own brand name is non-negotiable when OTAs bid on it; recommended ROAS floor before pausing
    - **OTA brand conquesting (if permitted):** Guidance on bidding against competitor OTA brand names and the "book direct" value proposition required (best rate guarantee, free cancellation, breakfast inclusion, room upgrade)
    - **Ad extensions for direct booking superiority:** Price extensions showing direct rate vs. OTA rate (if lower); promotion extensions for direct-only perks; callout extensions: "No booking fees," "Instant confirmation," "Loyalty points"
    - **Sample RSA headlines (3):** Brand name + "Official Site"; "Book Direct — Best Rate Guaranteed"; "No OTA Fees — [Loyalty Program] Points"
    - **Sample descriptions (2):** Direct booking benefit stack + free cancellation policy; Social proof stat + CTA to book

    Include a note on bidding rules: some OTA contracts prohibit conquesting — verify before launching.

    # Travel & Hospitality — Content & SEO Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Travel SEO is dominated by destination intent, comparison, and planning queries. The highest-converting content closes the aspiration-to-booking gap by matching the emotional frame of the search query (inspiration, comparison, preparation) with specific, credible, review-anchored content. Destination guides, seasonal planning articles, and pre-booking content hubs drive both organic traffic and direct booking lift.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. Destination Guide / City SEO Pillar Page

    Write an SEO destination guide page for a [hotel/resort/OTA/tour operator] targeting the keyword "[destination] travel guide" or "things to do in [destination]."

    Page structure:
    - **H1:** "The [Year] [Destination] Travel Guide: [Best Experiences / Hidden Gems / Where to Stay + What to Do]"
    - **Intro (100 words):** Sensory-vivid destination framing — what the destination feels like to first-time and returning visitors; hint at the angle this guide takes (luxury, adventure, family, cultural)
    - **Best time to visit (150 words):** Month-by-month breakdown — peak season (why it's popular, what to expect in crowds and price), shoulder season (best value window), and off-peak (what's still good, what to avoid)
    - **Where to stay (200 words):** 3–4 area or property-type options with brief descriptions; include a "For [traveler type]" framing for each
    - **Top experiences (5 items):** Each with a 60-word description using first-person sensory language ("you'll find yourself..." "the moment the boat rounds the bay...")
    - **Getting there + around:** Brief transport summary — arrival options and local navigation
    - **Internal links:** Link to property or tour booking page, seasonal package page, and review showcase

    Tone: Authoritative guide voice with genuine warmth. Must earn the reader's trust before it earns the booking.

    ---

    ### 2. Seasonal Travel Planning Article

    Write a seasonal travel content article for [destination] targeting the keyword "best time to visit [destination]" or "[destination] in [month/season]."

    Article structure:
    - **H1:** "Visiting [Destination] in [Month/Season]: What to Expect, What to Book, and What to Skip"
    - **Intro (80 words):** Does this season live up to the hype? Give an honest lead — not every season is ideal for every traveler.
    - **Weather + conditions (100 words):** Average temperatures, rainfall, crowd levels, and any seasonal event anchors (festivals, migrations, blossoms, ski conditions)
    - **What's best about this season (150 words):** 3 specific experiences that this season makes exceptional — with sensory framing, not generic descriptions
    - **What to watch out for (100 words):** Honest — price peaks, crowds, weather risks. Builds trust through honesty.
    - **Packing + preparation (80 words):** Season-specific practical advice
    - **Where to stay for this season (100 words):** 2–3 property or area recommendations calibrated to this season's conditions
    - **CTA:** Link to seasonal package or booking page; link to full destination guide

    ---

    ### 3. Property or Experience Comparison Page

    Write an SEO comparison page for a [hotel/resort/tour operator] targeting the keyword "[Property A] vs [Property B]" or "best [property type] in [destination area]."

    Page structure:
    - **H1:** "[Property A] vs [Property B]: Which is Right for Your [Destination] Trip?" or "The Best [Property Type] in [Area]: An Honest Comparison"
    - **Intro (100 words):** Frame who this comparison is for — type of traveler, travel style, budget range — so readers self-select
    - **Comparison table (6–8 rows):** Location, room types/views, dining, pool/beach access, amenities, price range, best for (traveler type)
    - **"Who should choose [Property A]" (80 words):** Specific profile — families, couples, first-timers, adventure seekers
    - **"Who should choose [Property B]" (80 words):** Honest alternative framing — even if you own Property A, honesty builds long-term trust
    - **Review snapshot for each:** Star rating + one representative guest quote per property
    - **CTA:** Direct booking link for your property; link to full destination guide

    Tone: Genuine comparison voice. Don't mock the competitor — earn preference through specificity.

    - Getting here (transport options, airport transfers, self-drive tips)
    - What to pack for [destination + season]
    - [Destination] dining guide (neighborhood by neighborhood, or property restaurant previews)
    - Local experiences: 5 things guests love near [property]
    - Property arrival guide (check-in time, parking, early arrival options)
    - [Property] FAQ (WiFi, concierge hours, beach/pool rules)

    Sample article (pick one hub section and write it in full):
    Write "What to Pack for [Destination] in [Season]" (400–500 words).
    - Lead with the packing challenge specific to this destination + season (humidity + dress code, altitude + layers, beach + cultural site combinations)
    - 4–6 packing categories with specific item recommendations
    - Close with a "What you don't need to pack" section (property provides X, destination has Y available locally)

    Tone: Helpful, insider-knowledgeable, warm. The reader trusts you — maintain that trust through genuinely useful specificity.

    # Travel & Hospitality — Lifecycle & Email Industry Overlay Prompts

    > **Context:** Travel email lifecycle spans a long pre-trip window (days to months) and a high-emotion post-trip window. The highest-performing sequences match the traveler's emotional state at each stage: excitement after booking, practical preparation closer to arrival, delight and gratitude post-trip, and nostalgia for re-engagement. Overly promotional email in the pre-trip phase erodes trust — prioritize experience enrichment over upsell frequency.

    ## Prompts

    ### 1. Post-Booking Confirmation and Excitement Sequence (Days 0–3)

    Write a 3-email post-booking sequence for a [hotel/resort/operator], starting immediately after a reservation is confirmed.

    Email 1 — Confirmation (send: immediately):
    - Subject: "You're going to [Destination]. Here's everything you need. [Booking ref]"
    - Body structure: Booking confirmation details (dates, room type, total); destination excitement hook (2 sentences — sensory, not generic); 3 immediate next steps (add to calendar, download app, contact concierge); CTA: "View your booking"

    Email 2 — Destination preview (send: 24–48 hours post-booking):
    - Subject: "[Destination] is waiting. Here's what guests love most."
    - Body structure: Top 3 guest-recommended experiences (with brief sensory descriptions); property highlight (pool, beach, or signature experience); optional upsell for room upgrade, early check-in, or dining package — positioned as an enhancement, not a sales push; CTA: "Explore your stay"

    Email 3 — Planning nudge (send: 72 hours post-booking, or 2 weeks before arrival if booked far in advance):
    - Subject: "A few things to know before you arrive in [Destination]."
    - Body structure: 3 practical preparation tips (visa, transport, local currency); link to pre-trip guide; soft CTA: "Any questions? Your concierge is ready."

    Tone: Warm, helpful, building anticipation. Not promotional. The reader has already committed — your job is to increase confidence and deepen excitement.

    ---

    ### 2. Pre-Travel Preparation Series (7–14 Days Before Arrival)

    Write a 2-email pre-travel series for a [hotel/resort/operator] targeting guests arriving in 7–14 days.

    Email 1 — Countdown + logistics (send: 14 days before):
    - Subject: "14 days until [Destination]. Your pre-trip checklist."
    - Body structure: Check-in time + early arrival options; transport to property (shuttle, taxi app, self-drive); local weather forecast context; link to full pre-trip guide; CTA: "Confirm your transfer" (if offered)

    Email 2 — Final preparation + on-property preview (send: 7 days before):
    - Subject: "One week to go. Here's what to expect when you arrive."
    - Body structure: Arrival process (check-in, parking, key handover); preview of 3 on-property experiences (dining reservation suggestion, spa booking link, activity sign-up); packing reminder for destination-specific items (sun protection, formal dress code if applicable); CTA: "Book your dinner reservation"

    Tone: Practical and reassuring. The closer to travel, the more concrete the help should be — reduce anxiety, increase anticipation.

    ---

    ### 3. Post-Trip Thank-You, Review Request, and Loyalty Upsell

    Write a 2-email post-trip series for a [hotel/resort/operator], starting 24–48 hours after check-out.

    Email 1 — Thank-you and review request (send: 24–48 hours post-checkout):
    - Subject: "Thank you for staying at [Property]. How was your experience?"
    - Body structure: Warm, genuine thank-you (2 sentences — specific to the destination/experience, not generic); review request with bold CTA (TripAdvisor and/or Google review link); low-friction format — explain it takes 2 minutes; optional feedback form for private comments (lets guests vent privately rather than publicly); 1 seasonal or returning-guest soft offer below the fold

    Email 2 — Return visit and loyalty (send: 3–7 days post-checkout):
    - Subject: "Come back to [Destination]. [Loyalty benefit or returning guest offer]."
    - Body structure: 1 highlight memory anchor ("Last week you were at [destination]..."); returning guest benefit or loyalty program enrollment; 1 seasonal next-visit hook (upcoming event, best next season); CTA: "Check availability" with pre-filled dates from their last stay duration

    Tone: Grateful and genuine. The review request must not feel transactional — frame it as helping other travelers, not benefiting the business.

    ---

    ### 4. Lapsed-Guest Win-Back Campaign

    Write a 2-email win-back campaign for a [hotel/resort/operator] targeting guests who stayed 12+ months ago and have not re-booked.

    Email 1 — Nostalgia + destination update (send to 12-month+ lapsed guests):
    - Subject: "It's been a while since [Destination]. A lot has changed."
    - Body structure: Warm callback to their previous stay (reference season and/or year — not individual room/booking data); 3 things that are new or improved since their last visit (renovation, new restaurant, new experience offering); no discount lead — earn the re-engagement before the offer

    Email 2 — Returning guest offer (send: 5–7 days after Email 1, if no click):
    - Subject: "A welcome-back offer for guests who know [Property]."
    - Body structure: Named returning-guest benefit (room upgrade, breakfast inclusion, or rate discount — choose one; stacking multiple discounts cheapens the brand); clear validity window (offer expires [date]); frictionless CTA: "Check availability for your dates"

    Tone: Warm and relationship-led. Avoid "we miss you" clichés. Ground the re-engagement in a genuine destination or property update.
    ```
