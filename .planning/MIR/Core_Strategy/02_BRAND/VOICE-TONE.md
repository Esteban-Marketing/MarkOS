# VOICE-TONE — MarkOS by esteban.marketing

## BRAND ARCHETYPE
Primary: Outlaw-Rebel (disrupts traditional agency and CMO models)
Secondary: Sage (empirically grounded, evidence-based, no fluff)
Tension: Operates at the intersection of disruptive speed and analytical precision

## TONE DIMENSIONS
Formal ←→ Casual: Casual — "Speak like you're in the room with the client; never use jargon for its own sake."
Serious ←→ Playful: Serious — "Every word has a job; avoid fluff, but don't perform for laughs."
Expert ←→ Accessible: Accessible — "Explain technical concepts in plain language, but never dumb it down."
Direct ←→ Diplomatic: Direct — "Say what matters, skip the preamble. If it's a risk, name it."
Emotional ←→ Rational: Rational — "Lead with evidence and outcomes, not hype or emotion."

## VOICE CHARACTERISTICS
### What MarkOS copy sounds like
- Leads with the result, not the process
  - Bad: "We use a multi-step methodology to develop your brand voice"
  - Good: "Your brand speaks consistently across every channel — from day one"
- Calls out industry BS directly
  - Example: "Most dashboards create more work than they save. MarkOS lives in your codebase and gets to work."
- Uses numbers and specifics, not generalities
  - Example: "Push 10 Linear tickets in 60 seconds — no manual entry."
- Short, punchy sentences
  - Example: "No-fluff. No dashboards. Just output."
- Empirical claims, always with a proof point
  - Example: "Every campaign is tracked to install, not just impressions."
- Action-oriented, always a next step
  - Example: "Install MarkOS. Run your first campaign. See the difference."
- Owns mistakes and gaps transparently
  - Example: "Feature X is in the backlog — see EST-42 for ETA."

### What MarkOS copy never sounds like
- Overly formal or academic
  - Bad: "Our proprietary methodology leverages cutting-edge AI to synergize your marketing."
- Vague or generic
  - Bad: "We help you grow."
- Hype-driven or exaggerated
  - Bad: "World-class, seamless, robust solutions for every business."
- Passive or apologetic
  - Bad: "We try our best to..."
- Process theater
  - Bad: "Our 7-step process ensures..."
- Feature overclaiming
  - Bad: "Everything is automated."
- Buzzword stacking
  - Bad: "Innovative, next-gen, AI-powered..."
- Flattery or empty praise
  - Bad: "You're amazing for being here!"

## SENTENCE STRUCTURE RULES
- First sentence of any piece must be ≤12 words and state the core claim
- Average sentence length: 10–14 words
- Paragraphs: Max 3 sentences
- Use lists for steps, features, or claims — avoid dense prose
- No more than one subordinate clause per sentence
- Use active voice exclusively
- Every section ends with a call to action or next step

## CHANNEL ADAPTATIONS
### LinkedIn (Esteban personal brand)
- Tone: Direct, empirical, slightly contrarian
- Format rules: Hook in first line, 1–2 line paragraphs, use data or proof in every post, CTA at end ("DM for install link" or "See how MarkOS runs in your codebase")
- What to avoid: Flattery, generic growth claims, overused LinkedIn tropes ("Let me tell you a story...")

### Cold Email / Outbound
- Tone: Direct, concise, outcome-focused
- Format rules: ≤5 lines, personalized first line, clear CTA ("Reply with 'MarkOS' for install link"), no more than one link per email

### Product UI / Onboarding
- Tone: Instructive, diagnostic, encouraging
- Format rules: Explain what happens next, not what the user did wrong. Use specific next steps.
