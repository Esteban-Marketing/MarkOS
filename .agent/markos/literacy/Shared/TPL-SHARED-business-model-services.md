# Services / Consulting Business Model

This tone doc extends `TPL-SHARED-overlay-consulting.md` with funnel-stage tactical breakdown for professional services and consulting businesses. Services selling is relationship-first and trust-dependent. Unlike product purchases, the buyer is committing to a working relationship — and likely facing a significant financial and organizational investment. Write accordingly.

## EVIDENCE BASE

Professional services buying is driven by a single question: "Can I trust this firm to deliver what they say they can deliver?" Multiple stakeholders are typically involved; the price point is high; and the consequence of a wrong choice is serious operations risk. This makes traditional marketing tactics (urgency, scarcity, promotional framing) actively counterproductive — they signal desperation or low-trust calibration.

The acquisition funnel for services is long by nature. Clients rarely convert from first touch. They research practitioners, read case studies, evaluate process transparency, and seek referrals before committing. Content and paid targeting must serve the research phase, not close at it.

## CORE TACTICS

### Awareness
- **Thought leadership as primary acquisition** — Content that demonstrates expertise on the problems the ICP is facing, not promotion of services
- **LinkedIn as the channel** — Professional services buyers research practitioners on LinkedIn; content and ads here reach the actual decision-maker population
- **Educational > promotional** — The rule of thumb: if the content could be published without any brand mention and still be valuable, it's the right awareness content
- **Problem framing over solution pushing** — "Here's how to think about [problem]" before "here's how we solve [problem]"

### Consideration
- **Case study as the currency** — Specific client context (industry, challenge, constraint), methodology applied, and measurable outcome. Anonymized is fine; vague is not.
- **Process transparency** — "Here's how we approach [type of engagement]" — shows the buyer exactly what they're signing up for, which reduces high-commitment hesitation
- **Comparison positioning** — Not against competitor firms, but against alternatives: "DIY vs. specialist," "hiring in-house vs. engaging us," "doing this now vs. delaying"
- **Proposal / pitch content** — Consideration-stage content should inform proposals, not replace them. Content earns the right to the conversation.

### Decision
- **Low-commitment CTA** — "Schedule a discovery call" or "Get a free [audit/assessment]" — not "buy now." The buyer is not ready to commit; they're ready to learn if this is a fit.
- **Proof-of-process CTAs** — "See how we've approached this for [industry]" → case study or methodology page. The click should lead to more confidence, not a form.
- **Reference availability** — For high-ticket engagements, offering client references is a trust differentiator. Content can signal this: "We're happy to connect you with past clients in your industry."

### Onboarding
- **Onboarding-as-reassurance** — The first 30 days confirm the client made the right choice. Content should support: what to expect, how to raise concerns, what success looks like early.
- **Stakeholder management** — Services purchases often involve multiple internal stakeholders. Onboarding materials should address the buyer and their internal team.

### Retention
- **Ongoing insight delivery** — Clients who receive ongoing value beyond the contract scope renew. Monthly check-in materials, relevant research, proactive recommendations.
- **Expansion through results** — Growth comes from "we completed scope X, here's what scope Y would unlock" — earned after delivering on the initial engagement, never pitched during it.

## COUNTER-INDICATORS

- **Urgency language of any kind** — "Limited spots," "Offer ends," "Don't miss out" — these signals actively reduce trust in professional services
- **Price anchoring in awareness content** — Pricing belongs in the proposal, not in ads or top-of-funnel content
- **Generic testimonials** — "It was great to work with them!" — says nothing. Case study proof or nothing.
- **Feature lists masquerading as differentiation** — "We offer strategy, execution, and reporting" is table stakes. Differentiate on methodology, results, or client type served.
- **Startup jargon or buzzword stacking** — "synergistic," "holistic solutions," "disruptive approach" — all register as low-credibility in senior buyer populations
